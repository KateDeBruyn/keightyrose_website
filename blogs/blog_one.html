McLuhan Reflection: Mediums as Messages

I feel that the McLuhan reading teaches us to think about how mediums become messages, how they influence those messages (in the methods a medium will use to communication a message), and possibly how those messages can even then become their own mediums. Mediums have social, political, economic, personal consequences - affecting our ideas of humanity. This reading made me think of Stuart Hall's systems of representation - how signifier and signified make up a sign and how these signs sometimes create a universal language, a message. Within this, mediums are signs which contain messages exclusive to the content of the medium. Adverts that appear on TV or radio indicate a message that the company can afford such an expense, possibly making them seem more reputable, as opposed to seeing a written advert in the classified section of a newspaper. There was a lot to unpack in the reading, hopefully we can briefly talk about it in next week's round table. But otherwise these were too comments that I thought I'd add to the discussion: His point on the American Revolution, that it "had no medieval legal institutions to discard or to root out, apart from monarchy", paired with his sentiment that England is yet to undergo their revolution, feels like it missed an opportunity to highlight the medieval legalities that the American Civil Rights movement aimed to root out. I know he's speaking on De Tocqueville's work and is focusing on technology but I feel this is important: The mediums of the movement, which I feel stretch all the way from posters to even schools (like in Little Rock), are such good examples of messages becoming mediums themselves. The protest at Little Rock Central Highschool can be encapsulated as a medium because it was a platform for white supremacists to spew their ideologies (their message), but the resulting message that came from their actions - black students being escorted by the military - became a medium for civil rights activists to further their message. I bring this up because McLuhan published this work in 1964, several years in to the CRM - a legal, political and social revolution in my opinion.

The grammar of print cannot help to construe the message of oral and nonwritten culture and institutions" (page 4) - this made me think of a reading on orality and oral cultures and how we learned about it in relation to storytelling - a spoken story (at first a message) told by a storyteller (arguably the medium) becomes itself a medium within the listener's memory. Remembering and recalling the relevance of the story (or the even storyteller) would then translate into a message that is relevant for the listeners current activity (like if they were talking to someone about a story they once heard). I feel the same process would happen with a written and read story, just that the messages would have different results. What this reading ultimately made me think of is what messages my future games will hold depending on the medium they are played through. That the medium, as "any extension of ourselves", shows the impact of scenarios like using mouse and keyboard as opposed to a controller. GTA and Rocket League, to me, are 100% better experienced through using a controller. Maybe that comes from my personal background (it probably does), but it's the same as preferring Sims on PC rather than PS2 or mobile. With regards to Sims, PC and mobile versions are almost totally different games. And they have these vast differences because they are being played on different mediums which limit and allow for different types of gameplay - that would be a cool essay to dive into.
